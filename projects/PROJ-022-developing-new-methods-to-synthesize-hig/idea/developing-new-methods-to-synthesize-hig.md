# Developing New Methods to Synthesize High-Performance Membranes using Sustainable Materials

**Field**: chemistry

## Research question

How can sustainable feedstocks be chemically engineered to produce membrane materials with permeability and selectivity comparable to conventional petrochemical-based polymers?

## Motivation

Conventional membrane production relies on non-renewable resources and generates significant waste during fabrication. A shift to sustainable materials is critical for environmental remediation and energy production, yet performance gaps hinder widespread adoption of bio-based alternatives.

## Related work

- [Benefits of Resource Strategy for Sustainable Materials Research and Development](http://arxiv.org/abs/1703.01840v1) — Discusses resource strategy aspects of essential raw materials impacting material applicability and life cycles.
- [Environmental sustainability in basic research: a perspective from HECAP+](http://arxiv.org/abs/2306.02837v2) — Highlights the responsibility of the scientific community to limit negative environmental impacts in research.
- [A Chemical Route to Graphene for Electronics and Spintronics Device Applications](http://arxiv.org/abs/1409.5849v1) — Demonstrates selective high precision chemical functionalization strategies for material fabrication.
- [Multi-Principal-Element Approach to High-Performance Thermoelectric Materials](http://arxiv.org/abs/1911.03797v1) — Explores high-entropy approaches for exploiting high-performance material properties via composition engineering.

## Expected results

Identification of 2-3 sustainable precursor combinations that yield membrane structures with >80% of the flux of commercial polyamide membranes. Evidence requires structural characterization (XRD, SEM) and permeability testing against standard dyes or gases to confirm selectivity.

## Methodology sketch

- **Data Acquisition**: Obtain raw biomass feedstocks (e.g., lignin, chitosan) from certified suppliers; no public datasets available for physical synthesis.
- **Synthesis**: Perform wet-lab polymerization and cross-linking reactions using standard glassware and thermal ovens (requires physical lab environment).
- **Fabrication**: Cast membranes via solution casting and dry in controlled humidity chambers.
- **Characterization**: Use Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and X-ray Diffraction (XRD) to analyze pore structure and crystallinity.
- **Testing**: Measure water flux and solute rejection using cross-flow filtration cells (requires physical instrumentation).
- **Analysis**: Compare performance metrics against commercial benchmarks using standard deviation analysis.
